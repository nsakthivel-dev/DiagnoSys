Rare Disease
Clinical Reference Report
Symptoms  |  Diagnosis  |  Treatment  |  Case Examples
This report covers 10 rare diseases with symptom profiles, standardised diagnostic pathways, treatment approaches, and illustrative anonymised case examples. All case examples are composite illustrations for educational purposes and do not represent any specific individual.
Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS)
Classification: Genetic / Connective Tissue
  Key Symptoms
Hypermobile or unstable joints prone to dislocation
Skin that stretches unusually far and feels velvety
Easy bruising and slow wound healing
Chronic widespread musculoskeletal pain
Fatigue and exercise intolerance
Autonomic dysfunction (POTS) in some subtypes
Digestive issues and gastric dysmotility
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Physiotherapy, pain management (NSAIDs, low-dose naltrexone), joint bracing, psychological support. No cure. Vascular EDS requires annual imaging.
  Prognosis
Variable by subtype. Most have normal lifespan; vascular EDS has serious complications. Quality of life management is ongoing.
Myasthenia Gravis (MG)
Classification: Autoimmune / Neuromuscular
  Key Symptoms
Ptosis (drooping eyelids), often worse later in the day
Diplopia (double vision)
Difficulty swallowing or speaking (bulbar symptoms)
Proximal limb weakness (arms, thighs)
Respiratory muscle weakness — myasthenic crisis in severe cases
Symptoms fluctuate and worsen with exertion or illness
Thymic abnormalities (thymoma in ~15% of patients)
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (pyridostigmine), corticosteroids, azathioprine, rituximab for refractory cases. Thymectomy if thymoma present. IVIg or plasmapheresis for crises.
  Prognosis
With treatment, most achieve significant symptom control. Remission possible post-thymectomy. Crisis requires ICU care.
Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)
Classification: Autoimmune / Hepatic
  Key Symptoms
Persistent fatigue (most common presenting complaint)
Pruritus (intense itching), especially at night
Jaundice in advanced disease
Dry eyes and dry mouth (sicca syndrome)
Hyperpigmentation of the skin
Portal hypertension in late-stage disease
Xanthelasma (cholesterol deposits around eyes)
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) first-line slows progression. Obeticholic acid (OCA) for non-responders. Cholestyramine for pruritus. Liver transplant in end-stage disease.
  Prognosis
Excellent if UDCA started early and liver function normalises. Late-stage patients risk cirrhosis. Transplant has high success rate.
Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)
Classification: Autoimmune / Acquired
  Key Symptoms
Ascending weakness starting in the feet and legs
Tingling, numbness, or burning pain in extremities
Progressive limb paralysis over days to weeks
Loss of deep tendon reflexes (areflexia)
Autonomic instability: blood pressure swings, arrhythmias
Facial weakness and swallowing difficulty in severe cases
Respiratory failure requiring ventilation in ~25% of cases
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
IVIg (0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days) or plasmapheresis — equivalent efficacy. Supportive care, physiotherapy, respiratory monitoring. No role for corticosteroids.
  Prognosis
Most recover over weeks to months. ~5% mortality. Residual weakness in ~20%. Relapses rare (CIDP if chronic).
Cystic Fibrosis (CF)
Classification: Genetic / Multi-system
  Key Symptoms
Thick, sticky mucus accumulation in lungs and airways
Recurrent pulmonary infections (Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus)
Bronchiectasis and progressive lung function decline
Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency — malabsorption, fatty stools
Poor weight gain and growth failure in childhood
Salty-tasting skin; high sweat chloride
Male infertility due to absent vas deferens (CBAVD)
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
CFTR modulators (elexacaftor/tezacaftor/ivacaftor — Trikafta) for eligible mutations. Airway clearance physiotherapy, pancreatic enzymes, high-calorie diet, inhaled antibiotics.
  Prognosis
Transformed by CFTR modulators. Patients on Trikafta have dramatically improved lung function and life expectancy. Early intervention critical.
Hereditary Hemochromatosis (HH)
Classification: Genetic / Metabolic
  Key Symptoms
Fatigue and chronic tiredness (often first symptom)
Joint pain, especially in index and middle finger MCP joints
Bronze or grey skin hyperpigmentation
Liver fibrosis and cirrhosis with elevated liver enzymes
Diabetes mellitus due to pancreatic iron deposition
Cardiac arrhythmias and cardiomyopathy
Hypogonadism and decreased libido in males
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Therapeutic phlebotomy (venesection) 450-500ml weekly until ferritin <50 ug/L, then maintenance every 3–4 months. Avoid vitamin C supplements and raw shellfish.
  Prognosis
Excellent if treated before cirrhosis or diabetes. Normal life expectancy achievable. End-organ damage partially reversible.
Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency (AATD)
Classification: Genetic / Pulmonary & Hepatic
  Key Symptoms
Progressive shortness of breath and exertional dyspnoea
Wheezing, chronic cough — frequently misdiagnosed as asthma or COPD
Early-onset emphysema, especially in the lower lung zones
Liver disease: neonatal cholestasis, cirrhosis in adults
Necrotising panniculitis — painful nodular skin lesions (rare)
Reduced exercise tolerance and recurrent chest infections
Symptoms worsen significantly with smoking
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Smoking cessation is critical. Standard COPD therapy (bronchodilators, inhaled steroids). Augmentation therapy (IV AAT protein infusions) in eligible patients with FEV1 35–65%.
  Prognosis
Non-smokers with ZZ genotype have near-normal life expectancy. Smokers fare significantly worse. Liver disease managed by transplant in severe cases.
Wilson's Disease
Classification: Genetic / Metabolic
  Key Symptoms
Hepatic: jaundice, hepatomegaly, acute liver failure in young patients
Tremors, dysarthria, and bradykinesia (Parkinsonian features)
Neuropsychiatric: mood swings, depression, psychosis
Kayser-Fleischer rings — golden-brown rings at corneal periphery
Haemolytic anaemia due to copper-induced red cell destruction
Renal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome)
Sunflower cataracts (copper deposits in the lens)
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
D-penicillamine or trientine (chelation therapy) to remove copper. Zinc acetate for maintenance and pre-symptomatic cases. Liver transplant for fulminant hepatic failure.
  Prognosis
Excellent if diagnosed early before irreversible organ damage. Neurological symptoms may take 18–24 months to improve. Lifelong treatment required.
Fabry Disease
Classification: Genetic / Lysosomal Storage (X-linked)
  Key Symptoms
Episodic severe burning pain in hands and feet (acroparesthesia)
Heat, cold, and exercise intolerance
Gastrointestinal: abdominal pain, diarrhoea, early satiety
Angiokeratomas — small dark red/purple skin lesions
Cornea verticillata (whorl-like corneal opacities on slit-lamp)
Progressive chronic kidney disease and proteinuria
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy and arrhythmias
Strokes and TIAs in young adults — often the presenting event
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Enzyme replacement therapy (ERT): agalsidase alfa or agalsidase beta IV every 2 weeks. Migalastat (chaperone therapy) for amenable mutations. Pain managed with gabapentin, carbamazepine.
  Prognosis
ERT slows progression but does not reverse established organ damage. Early treatment produces better outcomes. Females often under-treated — equal monitoring required.
Stiff Person Syndrome (SPS)
Classification: Autoimmune / Neurological
  Key Symptoms
Progressive muscle rigidity, especially in the trunk and abdomen
Painful, sudden muscle spasms triggered by noise, touch, or emotion
Hyperlordosis — extreme curvature of the lumbar spine
Anxiety, agoraphobia — triggered by fear of spasm in public
Episodic falls due to sudden loss of postural control
Hyperekplexia — exaggerated startle response
In variant SPS: limb-predominant or cerebellar involvement
  Diagnosis Pathway
  Treatment
Diazepam (high dose) or baclofen for muscle rigidity. IVIg or plasmapheresis for acute exacerbations. Rituximab for severe refractory cases. Psychological support critical.
  Prognosis
Chronic, progressive condition. No cure but symptoms manageable. Falls and progressive disability are main concerns. Paraneoplastic cases depend on cancer treatment.

 | Case Example
A 24-year-old woman presented with recurrent shoulder and knee dislocations since adolescence, along with unexplained fatigue and digestive issues. After visiting several specialists over 6 years without a diagnosis, a rheumatologist applied the Beighton Score (8/9) and arranged genetic testing confirming hEDS. She was referred to a multidisciplinary team including a physiotherapist and pain specialist. Over approximately 18 months of targeted physical therapy and pain management, her dislocation frequency decreased significantly and daily function improved. She continues routine monitoring and has managed independently since.

 | Case Example
A 55-year-old man developed tingling in both feet after a respiratory infection, progressing to leg weakness over 5 days. He was hospitalised when walking became impossible. CSF showed elevated protein with no white cells. Nerve conduction confirmed acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (AIDP). A 5-day course of IVIg was administered. He spent 3 weeks in hospital and 6 months in intensive physiotherapy. Walking was regained at month 4; near-full recovery was documented at 11 months. He has had no relapse in the 4 years since.

 | Case Example
An infant was flagged on newborn screening with elevated IRT; sweat chloride (72 mmol/L) and CFTR genetic testing confirmed CF (F508del/F508del). The child was enrolled in a specialist CF centre from infancy with regular physiotherapy, pancreatic enzyme replacement, and nutritional support. At age 12, she was commenced on elexacaftor/tezacaftor/ivacaftor (Trikafta) following eligibility confirmation. Within 4 months, her FEV1 improved by 18 percentage points and BMI normalised. She has had only one hospitalisation in 3 years on treatment, compared to 4 per year previously.

 | Case Example
A 42-year-old man was incidentally found to have elevated liver enzymes on a routine health check. Iron studies showed transferrin saturation of 78% and ferritin of 1,820 ug/L. HFE genotyping confirmed C282Y homozygosity. MRI liver showed moderate iron overload without cirrhosis. He underwent weekly phlebotomy; ferritin normalised to 45 ug/L after approximately 14 months and 28 venesections. He now attends for maintenance phlebotomy every 12 weeks. Liver enzymes normalised within 6 months, and he reports significant improvement in joint pain and energy levels.

A 38-year-old non-smoking woman was diagnosed with 'atypical asthma' and treated ineffectively for 6 years.  | Case Example
A 19-year-old male student presented to psychiatry with aggressive behaviour, personality change, and poor academic performance over 8 months. Liver enzymes were mildly elevated. A neurology referral was made and slit-lamp examination revealed Kayser-Fleischer rings. Ceruloplasmin was low (0.06 g/L) and 24-hour urine copper was markedly elevated. Liver biopsy confirmed copper overload. He was started on trientine chelation therapy. Psychiatric symptoms improved significantly over 18 months, liver function normalised by 12 months. He continues zinc maintenance therapy and is in full-time employment 4 years post-diagnosis.

 | Case Example
A 28-year-old woman with a 12-year history of unexplained burning foot pain, heat intolerance, and recurrent abdominal cramps had been evaluated by multiple specialists without diagnosis. After her brother was diagnosed with Fabry disease, she underwent GLA genetic testing confirming the familial mutation. Lyso-Gb3 was elevated. Renal function was mildly impaired. Over 3 years, her pain crises reduced from several per month to approximately 2 per year, renal function stabilised, and she reported greatly improved quality of life. She remains on treatment with regular cardiac, renal, and neurological monitoring.

 | Case Example
A 44-year-old female primary school teacher developed progressive stiffness in her back and abdomen over 8 months, with sudden muscle spasms triggered by loud noises or unexpected touch. She became unable to work and developed anxiety about leaving her home. Neurological assessment found continuous EMG activity; anti-GAD65 antibodies were elevated at 2,800 IU/mL. MRI was normal. She was started on diazepam and referred for IVIg therapy (monthly cycles). Over 18 months of combined treatment, spasm frequency halved, and she regained the ability to walk outdoors with a walking aid. She continues on maintenance diazepam and attends cognitive behavioural therapy for associated anxiety, with stable function at 3-year follow-up.
